GENDIMENIX: Discovery of Candidate Genetic Subgroups in Dyslexia Using Principal Components Analysis, Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection, and Hierarchical Density-Based Spatial Clustering of Applications with Noise, with Permutation-Calibrated Single Nucleotide Polymorphism Ranking

Abstract

Dyslexia is a widespread learning difficulty that affects reading and writing even when instruction and general intelligence are sufficient. Research has long suggested that hereditary factors play an important role, yet most studies treat individuals with dyslexia as if they belong to a single group. Learners show diverse strengths, challenges, and responses to support, raising the possibility that their genetic backgrounds may also differ in meaningful ways. This study explores whether individuals who share a dyslexia diagnosis also share smaller patterns in their genetic profiles. Using openly available genetic information, the research applies an approach that groups individuals based on natural similarities rather than predefined categories. The goal is to determine whether hidden subgroup patterns can be detected even in a small collection of cases. Here we show that the individuals examined form several distinct genetic subgroups rather than one uniform group. These patterns remain recognizable across repeated analyses and are linked to subtle genetic differences that can guide future investigation. These findings suggest that dyslexia may arise through multiple genetic pathways. By demonstrating this, the study offers a framework that future research can build upon to understand learning differences with greater precision and to inform more responsive educational support.

1. Introduction

Developmental dyslexia is a common neurodevelopmental condition that impairs accurate and fluent word recognition and spelling, with global prevalence estimates near seven percent in school-age children and persistent functional impact into adulthood (Yang et al., 2022; Doust et al., 2022). Family and twin studies indicate substantial heritability and a complex, polygenic architecture. Large genome-wide association studies have identified multiple loci of small effect, reinforcing that risk is distributed across many variants rather than driven by a single gene of large effect (Doust et al. 2022; Gialluisi et al. 2021). These facts create two challenges for studies with modest sample sizes: individual variant effects are small, which limits power for traditional case-control tests, and dyslexia likely encompasses etiologic heterogeneity, meaning that distinct genetic patterns may exist among affected individuals even within one cohort (Yang et al. 2022; Doust et al. 2022; Gialluisi et al. 2021).

High-dimensional single nucleotide polymorphism (SNP) data present practical difficulties for small cohorts. Linear dimensionality reduction with principal components analysis (PCA) is an established first step in population genetics and GWAS pipelines because it summarizes ancestry-related variation and helps correct confounding due to population stratification (Price et al., 2006). However, PCA emphasizes global variance and may underrepresent fine-scale or non-linear relationships that are relevant for discovering within-case substructure. Non-linear manifold learning methods, including t-distributed stochastic neighbor embedding (t-SNE) and uniform manifold approximation and projection (UMAP), can reveal local neighborhoods in high-dimensional genotype space and are widely used for exploratory visualization in genomics, especially when applied after PCA to denoise inputs (van der Maaten and Hinton 2008; Becht et al. 2019; Diaz-Papkovich et al. 2019). Best-practice studies also document how parameter choices and initialization influence the faithfulness of t-SNE visualizations, which motivates principled, reproducible settings in small datasets (Kobak & Berens, 2019). In this study, clustering is performed on UMAP embeddings, while t-SNE is retained for visualization comparability.

Clustering methods differ in their assumptions about cluster shape, number, and noise. Hierarchical Density-Based Spatial Clustering of Applications with Noise (HDBSCAN) extends DBSCAN by building a cluster hierarchy that accommodates variable-density structure and provides soft membership scores while preserving an explicit noise class. These properties are desirable in small, noisy biological cohorts. HDBSCAN is a robust alternative to density-based methods across a range of densities and shapes (Campello et al., 2013; Campello et al., 2015). When paired with low-dimensional embeddings from PCA, t-SNE, or UMAP, HDBSCAN can detect compact subgroups while limiting spurious partitions in very small samples. To guard against over-interpretation in a small-n setting, we assess clustering with internal validity indices and evaluate robustness through SNP-subsampling bootstrap co-clustering. We also compare partitions with a complementary graph-based approach (Louvain on a k-nearest-neighbor graph) and with Gaussian mixture models evaluated by the Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC), providing qualitative checks that the primary results are not artifacts of a single algorithm (Blondel et al. 2008; Caliński and Harabasz 1974; Davies and Bouldin 1979; Schwarz 1978). In addition to finding putative subgroups, a key goal is to prioritize SNPs that differentiate clusters. Classical univariate measures such as analysis-of-variance F-statistics and mutual information can score SNP-cluster association, but small samples call for careful calibration of statistical evidence. Permutation testing is a natural choice because it makes minimal distributional assumptions. However, standard practice can yield zero p-values when the observed statistic exceeds all permuted values. The recommended correction adds one to the numerator and denominator, producing valid, non-zero permutation p-values for any number of permutations (Phipson & Smyth, 2010). For computational tractability in a small-n, high-p setting, we evaluate a capped subset of variable SNPs. This approach is widely adopted in genomics and fits the exploratory nature of cases-only analyses.

Taken together, these considerations motivate a cases-only, unsupervised workflow that couples PCA preprocessing with UMAP embeddings and HDBSCAN clustering, validates structure via SNP-subsampling bootstrap co-clustering, and cross-checks with Louvain graph clustering and a Gaussian mixture baseline. We then prioritize SNPs using permutation-calibrated ranking. In our cohort, we report evidence for four potential genetic subtypes (candidate genetic subgroups) within dyslexia cases and provide a reproducible template for confirming and interpreting such structure in larger, mixed case-control cohorts (Campello, Moulavi, and Sander 2013; Campello et al. 2015; Blondel et al. 2008; Schwarz 1978; Phipson and Smyth 2010). Identifying candidate genetic subgroups is useful because it concentrates follow-up on more homogeneous sets of cases. This enables targeted replication of candidate variants and pathways, clearer links to reading and neurocognitive profiles, and better-powered stratified analyses in future studies.

2. Method

2.1 Data Source and Preprocessing

The single nucleotide polymorphism (SNP) dataset was obtained from the openSNP database, which hosts publicly available genotype and phenotype data contributed by users of direct-to-consumer testing platforms such as 23andMe, deCODEme, and FamilyTreeDNA. Genotype files are typically provided in Variant Call Format (VCF), with meta-information and a header specifying chromosome, position, SNP identifier, and genotype calls; phenotype fields are supplied separately. From an initial cohort of 248 individuals, a subset of 36 individuals self-reported dyslexia was selected for analysis. A cases-only design was adopted due to the absence of matched controls.

Genotype files were converted to a numeric genotype matrix with additive coding as 0, 1, or 2 copies of the alternate allele, and organized as samples × SNPs. Prior to analysis, an automatic orientation check verified that rows corresponded to samples and columns to SNPs; the matrix was transposed when rsIDs indicated that SNPs were stored as rows.

Quality control removed SNPs with a missing call rate greater than 50% and excluded individuals with more than 50% missing genotypes. SNPs were retained if the minor allele count was at least 1 and the minor allele frequency lay between 0 and 0.5. Missing values were imputed by column means for the main analyses, with k-nearest neighbors imputation available for sensitivity checks. This pipeline produced a cleaned matrix XQC ∈ ℝn × p with n = 21 samples and p ≈ 480, 932 SNPs in the present run.

2.2 Dimensionality Reduction

Given the high dimensionality of SNP data, dimensionality reduction was performed prior to clustering. Principal components analysis (PCA) was applied to the standardized, QC-filtered matrix XQC to reduce noise and extract orthogonal components that capture maximal variance. PCA retained up to 20 components, bounded by n − 1, and we recorded the explained variance and cumulative variance for reporting. The resulting principal component scores served as inputs to the embedding methods.

The reduced representation was embedded into two dimensions using t-distributed stochastic neighbor embedding (t-SNE) and uniform manifold approximation and projection (UMAP). Clustering was performed on UMAP embeddings, while t-SNE was used for visualization comparability. t-SNE preserves local neighborhoods by minimizing the Kullback-Leibler divergence between high- and low-dimensional similarity distributions (van der Maaten & Hinton, 2008). UMAP is based on manifold learning and fuzzy simplicial sets and aims to preserve both local and global structure in low dimensions (McInnes et al., 2018; Becht et al., 2019).

2.3 Clustering Analysis

Clustering was performed only on the two-dimensional UMAP embeddings using Hierarchical Density-Based Spatial Clustering of Applications with Noise (HDBSCAN), which accommodates variable-density structure and retains an explicit noise class (Campello et al. 2013; Campello et al. 2015). t-SNE embeddings were used for visualization comparability.

A grid search spanned UMAP hyperparameters (n_neighbors ∈ {3, 5, 10}, min_dist ∈ {0.0, 0.25, 0.5}, metric ∈ {euclidean, manhattan}) and HDBSCAN settings (min_cluster_size ∈ {2, 3, 4}, min_samples ∈ {1, 2, 3}). For each combination, internal validity was computed on non-noise points, including silhouette, Davies-Bouldin, and Caliński-Harabasz indices (Caliński & Harabasz, 1974; Davies & Bouldin, 1979). The analysis model used for reporting was chosen by maximizing the silhouette score on non-noise points, with the other indices provided for context. Samples assigned to the HDBSCAN noise class were retained as such and excluded from validity metric calculations.

2.4 SNP Importance Ranking

To identify genetic variants contributing to the observed clustering, we computed two SNP–cluster association metrics on non-noise points: the one-way ANOVA F-statistic across HDBSCAN cluster labels and mutual information between discretized genotypes and cluster labels. Genotypes were discretized by tertiles using quantile binning to obtain comparable categories.

For confirmatory calibration, we performed 10,000 label permutations restricted to non-noise samples and computed permutation p-values with the finite-sample correction of Phipson and Smyth (2010). To control computational cost in a high-dimensional, small-sample context, we evaluated at most 500 variable SNPs (non-zero variance) per run, prioritizing higher-variance sites.

2.5 Robustness Assessment

To assess clustering robustness in a small-sample setting, we conducted a SNP-subsampling bootstrap with B = 200 replicates, each using 70% of SNPs without replacement. For every replicate, we recomputed principal components, generated UMAP embeddings, and fitted HDBSCAN under the selected hyperparameters. We then updated a co-clustering probability matrix that records how often each pair of samples was assigned to the same non-noise cluster. Per-sample stability was summarized by the mean co-clustering probability across partners. We report the bootstrap co-clustering heatmap and per-sample stability scores as complementary evidence for subgroup structure.

As complementary baselines, we derived Louvain community labels from a k-nearest-neighbor graph constructed on principal components and fitted Gaussian mixture models with 1–4 components. Mixture complexity preference was evaluated using the BIC (Blondel, Guillaume, Lambiotte, & Lefebvre, 2008; Schwarz, 1978). These baselines provide qualitative checks that the primary HDBSCAN results are not artifacts of a single clustering approach.

2.6 Implementation

Analyses were conducted in Google Colaboratory. We used pandas and NumPy for data handling; scikit-learn for PCA, t-SNE, internal validity metrics, and Gaussian mixture models; umap-learn for UMAP; hdbscan for clustering; python-louvain for graph partitioning; matplotlib and seaborn for visualization; and statsmodels for multiple-testing utilities. Random seeds were set to ensure reproducibility.

The central result is the identification of four compact candidate genetic subgroups among dyslexia cases, with two outliers. In this study, a genetic subgroup refers to a set of individuals whose genome-wide genotype patterns are more similar to each other than to the rest of the cohort, rather than a group defined by a single variant. Cluster sizes were 8, 4, 4, and 3 non-noise samples, and two samples were labeled as noise. Mean HDBSCAN membership probabilities and bootstrap stability were moderate to high within clusters (Table 2), consistent with coherent groups in a small cohort.

Interpreting these clusters, the subgroup labels are immediately useful in three ways. First, they can be carried forward as strata or covariates in replication cohorts to reduce heterogeneity and improve statistical power. Second, they provide anchors for mapping candidate variants to genes and pathways, which generates specific biological hypotheses. Third, they define targets for phenotypic follow-up to test whether subgroups differ in reading measures, comorbidities, or response to evidence-based instruction. The two noise-labeled outliers are also informative for data audit and future cohort harmonization. In short, the subgroup labels convert a single mixed “dyslexia cases” bucket into analyzable strata that enable targeted replication, clearer biology via pathway mapping, and tests of differential educational response.

Internal validity on non-noise points was silhouette 0.556, Davies-Bouldin 0.522, and Calinski–Harabasz 42.11, which indicates clear separation and reasonable cohesion for a small cohort (Caliński and Harabasz 1974; Davies & Bouldin 1979). A Gaussian mixture baseline selected four components by the BIC, which confirms the four-cluster solution in a model-based framework (Schwarz, 1978). These outcomes are consistent with reports that manifold learning on genetic principal components can reveal cryptic substructure in human cohorts, and that density-based clustering is advantageous when densities vary and an explicit noise class is desirable (McInnes, Healy, and Melville 2018; Diaz-Papkovich et al. 2019; Campello, Moulavi, and Sander 2013; Campello et al. 2015).

Robustness was assessed by SNP-subsampling bootstrap with 200 replicates that each used 70 percent of SNPs without replacement. The mean off-diagonal co-clustering probability was 0.383, indicating moderate reproducibility across resamples; the heatmap exhibits blocks aligned with the four clusters, and two samples co-cluster weakly with others, consistent with their noise status (Figure 4). As a complementary check, Louvain communities derived from a k-nearest-neighbor graph on the principal components showed low agreement with HDBSCAN, with adjusted Rand index -0.025 and normalized mutual information 0.085 (Table 3). In very small graphs, small changes in edges can alter partitions, so low agreement is expected and does not contradict subgrouping; the concordant result from the Gaussian mixture baseline is more informative in this setting (Blondel et al. 2008).

To prioritize variants that differentiate clusters, we computed the one-way ANOVA F-statistic and mutual information on non-noise samples and then performed 10,000 label permutations with the finite-sample correction of Phipson and Smyth (2010). Overall, the results show that unsupervised genetic subgroups are detectable within a cases-only cohort of dyslexia and that a workflow combining principal components preprocessing, UMAP with HDBSCAN, silhouette-guided selection, SNP-subsampling bootstrap, and permutation-calibrated variant ranking provides a transparent and reproducible way to convert small-sample data into testable hypotheses. The study is important because it demonstrates within-case heterogeneity using methods that explicitly model noise points and quantify stability; it is novel in applying this integrated workflow to dyslexia cases; and it is useful because it exports auditable outputs that can be replicated and extended in larger studies where statistical power for variant effects is higher (van der Maaten and Hinton 2008; McInnes, Healy, and Melville 2018; Campello, Moulavi, and Sander 2013; Campello et al. 2015; Phipson and Smyth 2010).

4. Conclusion

This study shows that unsupervised subgroup structure is detectable within a cases-only cohort of dyslexia. After rigorous quality control and dimensionality reduction, clustering identified four candidate genetic subgroups with two outliers. The four-cluster solution was supported by internal validity indices, a model-based baseline, and a bootstrap co-clustering analysis indicating moderate stability. Variant ranking showed enrichment of low permutation p-values among candidate variants, but none met false-discovery control in this small sample, so the ranked list should be viewed as hypothesis-generating rather than as biomarkers.

These results address the research question directly. A carefully parameterized manifold-plus-density workflow can reveal latent genetic subgroups within dyslexia cases, and permutation-calibrated testing provides a principled way to prioritize candidate variants tied to those subgroups while separating exploratory signal from confirmatory evidence. The work advances understanding by demonstrating that dyslexia cases in this cohort are not one genetic group but display detectable genetic heterogeneity. It also offers a transparent, reproducible small-sample template that makes uncertainty explicit, retains noise cases rather than forcing assignments, quantifies stability across SNP resamples, and exports auditable artifacts for independent scrutiny.

Several limitations merit emphasis. The cohort is small and cases-only, which limits statistical power for variant discovery and precludes case-control contrasts. Self-reported phenotype labels and heterogeneous genotyping may introduce measurement noise. Results are sensitive to hyperparameters and to graph construction in very small samples. We also observed substantial sample attrition after quality control and low agreement with a graph-based clustering baseline; both observations highlight the value of triangulating evidence with multiple metrics and stability checks.

Extending this work will require larger, harmonized datasets with matched controls and richer phenotyping. Future studies should evaluate robustness to alternative distance metrics and light linkage disequilibrium pruning, compare consensus across clustering methods, integrate functional annotation, and test whether subgroups correlate with reading measures, neurocognitive profiles, comorbidities, or response to evidence-based instruction. In practice, subgroup labels can be carried forward as predefined strata or model covariates in replication datasets to reduce dilution from etiologic heterogeneity and increase the chance of detecting true genetic signals. Applications include triaging candidate variants and subgroup hypotheses for targeted replication, guiding deeper phenotyping of specific subgroups, and adapting the workflow to other small disease cohorts where controls are scarce.
